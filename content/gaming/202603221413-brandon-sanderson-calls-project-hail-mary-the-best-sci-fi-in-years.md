The Cosmere author recommends a hit book while you wait for his next one

## Even Brandon Sanderson thinks Project Hail Mary is modern sci-fi at its best

   ![A detail from the book cover of Project Hail Mary, showing a spacesuited astronaut in freefall in space, against the backdrop of a yellow and black striped planet](https://static0.polygonimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/projecthailmary.jpg?&fit=crop&w=1200&h=675)Image: Ballantine Books

During [a recent stream of](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qcSVWagOosQ) _[Halo 2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qcSVWagOosQ)_ (obviously!) fantasy heavyweight and master geek-culture commentator [Brandon Sanderson](https://www.polygon.com/brandon-sanderson-imposter-syndrome-perfect-state/) took a moment to remind his audience that the sci-fi and fantasy world extends well beyond his own sprawling [Cosmere setting](https://www.polygon.com/brandon-sanderson-no-elves-cosmere/). While fans wait for his next big release, [_The Fires of December_](https://amzn.to/4lKIIFE), Sanderson rattled off a list of authors worth reading in the meantime, including Brent Weeks (the Lightbringer series), Fonda Lee ([The Green Bone saga](https://amzn.to/4dxBXVs)), Terry Pratchett ([Discworld](https://amzn.to/4lE5hvy)), and [Andy Weir of _The Martian_](https://www.polygon.com/project-hail-mary-movie-vs-book-changes-interview/) fame. Of Weir’s latest novel, [_Project Hail Mary_](https://amzn.to/3NOAgZq), Sanderson didn’t hedge: it’s his “favorite science fiction novel of recent years,” he said while gunning down Covenant hordes.

The timing might feel convenient, given [the long-anticipated movie version hitting theaters](https://www.polygon.com/project-hail-mary-review-ryan-gosling/), but Sanderson’s enthusiasm isn’t new or performative. Years before Hollywood came calling, he went out of his way to spotlight the book, even breaking from his usual Goodreads-only policy to record a full video review.

It’s not super surprising that he digs _Project Hail Mary_; Sanderson has built a career out of creating intricate magic systems treated with the rigor of science, and Weir operates in a similar space from the opposite direction, turning hard science into blockbuster storytelling. In his review, Sanderson makes clear that Weir’s book isn’t just good, it’s a synthesis of what modern science fiction can do — and by contrast to a lot of what's out there, it's a breath of fresh air. Here's his big takeaway:

> I really like The Martian. I like \[Weir's novel\] _Artemis_. And I like this better than either of those. This is more a Brandon book than... I mean I love, The Martian, but who doesn't love The Martian? This one has a little bit more science fiction to it. This one has a bit of a more complex narrative and a bit of a more interesting, complex character. And yet it has all the stuff I loved in _The Martian_. This is the stuff that Andy Weir is really good at. What can we learn as writers from Andy’s writing? What I love that Andy does is, he shows that optimism can be compelling as a narrative.

In a genre often dominated by grimdark worlds and dystopian collapse, _Project Hail Mary_ leans the other way for Sanderson, toward curiosity and problem-solving. Even when the stakes are enormous, the story never loses its sense of momentum or accessibility, blending hard science with a tone that invites rather than intimidates. Structurally, Sanderson singles out the novel’s use of the “white room” trope — a protagonist waking without memory and gradually piecing together their situation — as a particular highlight.

Whatever your personal take on Brandon Sanderson (and if online SFF discourse is any indication, there are plenty of opposing takes) his role as a champion of other writers is hard to dispute. Beyond glowing recommendations, he’s invested directly in the ecosystem, bringing authors into his business ventures and supporting them as an editor and publisher. He doesn’t just talk about uplifting the genre; he actively does it.

As for Andy Weir, the admiration doesn’t _exactly_ flow both ways — at least not publicly. A devoted hard sci-fi traditionalist, Weir tends to point readers toward classics when asked for recommendations. [His go-tos include](https://reason.com/2022/11/15/andy-weir-is-looking-forward-to-the-space-boom/) _I, Robot_ by Isaac Asimov and _The Moon Is a Harsh Mistress_ by Robert A. Heinlein.

Between Sanderson’s modern favorites and Weir’s foundational picks, one thing is clear: If you’re waiting on the next Cosmere installment, you’ve got plenty of reading to keep you busy.
