One of [the coolest laptops we saw at CES in January](https://www.theverge.com/tech/858897/ces-2026-best-laptops-asus-dell-acer-msi-hp-lenovo-concepts) was the new Dell XPS 16, with a unique 1–120Hz variable refresh rate display that can sip power when you don’t need the screen to stay speedy.

Just how little power might it consume? [_Notebookcheck_ has tested](https://www.notebookcheck.net/Panther-Lake-XPS-16-is-so-efficient-it-draws-just-1-5-W-when-idling-for-insanely-long-battery-life.1255734.0.html) a version of the laptop with that LG Display screen and a new [Intel Panther Lake chip](https://www.theverge.com/tech/867214/intel-core-ultra-x9-panther-lake-388h-laptop-cpu-review) — and it appears to be the most efficient laptop that’s _ever_ gone through its Wi-Fi web browsing test. At idle, the Core Ultra 325 laptop drew as little as 1.5 watts, and lasted nearly 27 hours of web browsing despite only housing a 70 watt-hour pack. That’s well shy of the 99.5Wh Dell has sometimes crammed into its 16-inch models.

That’s more battery life than _Notebookcheck_ has gotten out of any MacBook or MacBook Pro, and apparently [more than all but two other laptops](https://www.notebookcheck.net/Benchmarks-and-Test-Results.142793.0.html?type=&sort=&model_class=-1&month=36&archive=1&condensed=0&id=0&perfrating=0&or=0&condensed=0&showCount=0&showBars=1&showPercent=0&settings_class_array[]=1&settings_class_array[]=2&settings_class_array[]=3&settings_class_array[]=4&settings_class_array[]=6&settings_class_array[]=7&bench_307_846=1&model=1&cpu_name=1&screen_panel_type=1&battery_capacity=1&daysold=1) since it started running this test in 2014. And of those two laptops, [one relied](https://www.notebookcheck.net/More-than-24-hrs-battery-life-for-less-than-1-000-Lenovo-ThinkBook-16-G7-QOY-laptop-review.958162.0.html) on a Qualcomm Snapdragon X Plus chip, a larger 84Wh battery, and a mere 60Hz screen — while [the other](https://www.notebookcheck.net/Built-for-extremes-Durabook-S14I-industrial-laptop-review.1011275.0.html) had _two_ batteries for a total of 149Wh and a 60Hz screen as well.

I should caution you that we typically see much less battery life in an actual workday than we do in fixed battery life tests. But compared to other laptops, this Dell + Intel + LG Display combo seems like the new battery life champ. Note that Dell also sells it with a higher-res tandem OLED screen, though. To get the best battery life, you’ll need to settle for 1920 x 1200, no OLED, and no touchscreen.

While Dell may deserve a lot of credit as the system integrator, this tech may not be exclusive to Dell for long. [LG Display announced](https://news.lgdisplay.com/en/2026/03/lg-display-becomes-worlds-first-to-mass-produce-1-120hz-laptop-panel/) that it’s become the first in the world to mass-produce a 1–120Hz laptop LCD panel (which it’s branding as Oxide 1Hz), and plans to mass-produce an OLED version in 2027. Intel, too, isn’t just working with one display vendor: last October, [it announced it was working with Chinese panel maker BOE](https://www.pcmag.com/news/intel-boe-introduce-1hz-laptop-refresh-rate-cutting-power-use-by-up-to) on 1Hz refresh rate computers too.

Similar display tech has been in our smartwatches and smartphones for a while, by the way: [2019’s Apple Watch Series 5](https://www.theverge.com/2019/9/18/20869098/apple-watch-series-5-always-on-screen-features-price-best-smartwatch) introduced a 1-60Hz screen to use less power, I believe [2021’s Samsung Galaxy S21 Ultra](https://www.theverge.com/2021/1/27/22251977/samsung-display-ltpo-display-power-efficient-panel-galaxy-s21-ultra-debut) was the first with a 10-120Hz screen (though I’ve seen reports it only went down to 48Hz in practice) and that year’s [OnePlus 9 Pro went down to 1Hz](https://www.theverge.com/22344840/oneplus-9-pro-review-price-camera-screen-specs), and Apple added 1-120Hz to pro iPhones [in 2022](https://www.theverge.com/23320405/apple-iphone-14-pro-max-always-on-display-screen-feature).
