In a sea of massive valuations for early-stage AI startups, today we have a bit of rare news: a jumbo round and valuation step-up for an e-commerce company. Quince [announced](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/quince-raises-500m-series-e-resulting-in-10-1b-valuation-to-accelerate-the-manufacturer-to-consumer-platform-302710298.html) on Wednesday that it raised a $500 million Series E round at a $10.1 billion valuation.

The round was led by previous investor Iconiq, which also led Quince’s $200 million Series D in early 2025 at [a reported $4.5 billion valuation.](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-28/instagram-famous-retailer-quince-raises-cash-at-over-4-5-billion-value) That’s more than double the valuation in less than a year.

Quince rose to fame on [Instagram with its $50 cashmere sweater](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DSwCJK_DFXz/), but has since amassed a wider range of product offerings, including apparel, home, accessories, beauty, and wellness. Unlike typical e-commerce retail sites, the company manufactures its products and sells them to consumers directly.

Quince, [which launched out of beta in 2020](https://techcrunch.com/2020/10/12/quince-launches-out-of-beta-with-new-manufacturer-to-customer-model/), calls its business model “manufacturer-to-consumer.” And because it owns most of its own tech stack and controls its designs and manufacturing, Quince can more accurately predict its sales, according to a [blog post](https://www.iconiq.com/growth/insights/tripling-down-on-quince-retail-rebuilt-from-the-supply-chain-up) by Iconiq. This allows smaller batch manufacturing with less waste.

Quince and its investors argue that, unlike fast fashion, Quince can produce higher-quality products at low costs.

Not that the company has been without controversy. It has faced several lawsuits from brands alleging Quince is selling dupes of their designs. [Coach parent Tapestry is suing](https://madan-law.com/coach-sues-quince-for-trade-dress-infringement-over-copycat-bags/), as is Williams Sonoma, [Puck reported](https://puck.news/quince-v-deckers-is-suing-over-dupes-anticompetitive/). Deckers also sued over footwear designs, but [a court ruled in Quince’s favor](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/court-affirms-quince-designs-are-classic-not-copies-302590779.html).

If such scuffles have given Quince a copycat reputation, as Puck describes, the site’s customers are apparently unphased. The company says that its top-line revenue has now surpassed $1 billion. In January, it also [expanded to Canada](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/quince-is-finally-in-canadaand-high-quality-luxury-essentials-just-got-more-affordable-302663828.html).
