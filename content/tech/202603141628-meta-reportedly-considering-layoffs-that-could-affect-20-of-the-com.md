Meta is considering major layoffs that could affect 20% or more of the company’s workforce, [according to Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/meta-planning-sweeping-layoffs-ai-costs-mount-2026-03-14/).

These layoffs could help the Facebook parent company offset its [aggressive spending on AI infrastructure](https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/30/meta-to-spend-up-to-72b-on-ai-infrastructure-in-2025-as-compute-arms-race-escalates/), as well as [AI-related acquisitions and hiring](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/17/sam-altman-says-meta-tried-and-failed-to-poach-openais-talent-with-100m-offers/). Meta employed nearly 79,000 people as of December 31, according to a recent filing.

“This is speculative reporting about theoretical approaches,” a Meta spokesperson said in a statement.

The report comes as many tech companies — [most recently Block](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/26/jack-dorsey-block-layoffs-4000-halved-employees-your-company-is-next/) — have announced sweeping layoffs that they say are necessary as AI automates more work. But some pundits, and [even executives like OpenAI’s Sam Altman](https://www.sfchronicle.com/tech/article/layoffs-sam-altman-ai-washing-21647451.php), have suggested that many of these cuts are “[AI-washing](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/01/ai-layoffs-or-ai-washing/),” where executives [use AI as cover for other issues](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-13/the-ai-washing-of-job-cuts-is-corrosive-and-confusing), such as over-hiring during the pandemic.

The last time Meta announced layoffs of this scale was in November 2022, when it [cut 11,000 jobs](https://techcrunch.com/2022/11/09/meta-confirms-11000-layoffs-amounting-to-13-of-its-workforce/), followed by [another 10,000 in March 2023](https://techcrunch.com/2023/03/14/meta-to-cut-another-10000-jobs-zuckerberg-says/).
