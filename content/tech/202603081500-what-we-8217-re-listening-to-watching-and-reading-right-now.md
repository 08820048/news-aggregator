    ## [Mabe Fratti makes irresistible genre-hopping experimental cello pop](https://www.theverge.com/column/890713/mabe-fratti-sentir-que-no-sabes-review)
    
    ![mabe fratti cover](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/03/mabe-fratti-cover.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C8.3333333333333%2C100%2C83.333333333333&w=2400)
    
    ![mabe fratti cover](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/03/mabe-fratti-cover.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C8.3333333333333%2C100%2C83.333333333333&w=2400)
    
    Image: Tin Angel Records
    
    The opening notes of “Kravitz”, which kicks off Mabe Fratti’s 2024 record [_Sentir Que No Sabes_](https://tinangelrecords.bandcamp.com/album/sentir-que-no-sabes)_,_ are lodged in my brain permanently. It’s not a showy album, by any means. But there’s something about the buzzing of her cello, plucked as you might an upright bass. The way they ring out before coming to an abrupt stop, fuzz still hanging in the air, set against a simple kick and snare sat firmly in the pocket. There’s something industrial about the way it all comes together, like a jazzy “Closer.”
    
    Then come Fratti’s paranoid lyrics in Spanish about ears in the ceiling and someone listening through the walls, and the slightly atonal horn blasts. In the back half, the arrangement blooms with big piano chords, and the drums pick up steam. It’s the perfect opening to a record that sees Fratti taking her experimental impulses and working them into something that more closely resembles pop music, straying further from her avant-garde roots.

    ## [Laurie Spiegel’s The Expanding Universe is a masterpiece of early ambient music](https://www.theverge.com/entertainment/882915/laurie-spiegel-the-expanding-universe-review-masterpiece-ambient-music)
    
    ![the expanding universe](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/02/the-expanding-universe.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C13.248553240741%2C100%2C74.074074074074&w=2400)
    
    ![the expanding universe](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/02/the-expanding-universe.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C13.248553240741%2C100%2C74.074074074074&w=2400)
    
    Image: Laurie Spiegel
    
    I recently had the pleasure of interviewing [Laurie Spiegel](https://www.theverge.com/report/879819/laurie-spiegel-is-celebrating-40-of-music-mouse-with-a-modern-revival) for the site. As preparation for the interview, I spent a lot of time over the last couple of weeks revisiting Spiegel’s records, most notably [_The Expanding Universe_](https://lauriespiegel.bandcamp.com/album/the-expanding-universe), her 1980 masterpiece that blends synth experimentalism with early examples of what would eventually be called [ambient music](https://www.theverge.com/22175193/ambient-music-2020-pandemic-quarantine-at-home), and algorithmic composition techniques. It’s a marvel that sounds both nostalgic and cutting-edge at the same time.
    
    Tracks like “Patchwork” and “A Folk Study” dabble in the sort of bouncy arpeggios that beg comparisons to The Who’s “Baba O’Riley,” while “Old Wave” and “East River Dawn” conjure [early M83](https://www.theverge.com/column/871847/m83-dead-cities-red-seas-lost-ghosts) or Boards of Canada. The palette she draws from is buzzing with life and timeless, rarely dating itself in the way her later (also excellent) record [_Unseen Worlds_](https://lauriespiegel.bandcamp.com/album/unseen-worlds) does, as it occasionally dabbles in FM bells.

    ## [M83’s icy Dead Cities, Red Seas & Lost Ghosts is the perfect blizzard soundtrack](https://www.theverge.com/column/871847/m83-dead-cities-red-seas-lost-ghosts)
    
    ![m83 dead cities](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/02/m83-dead-cities.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C4.1666666666667%2C100%2C91.666666666667&w=2400)

    New York City got hit with a hell of a snowstorm last week. And, inevitably, when I’m watching the snow fall, wandering the oddly quiet streets after dark, people hiding inside and staying warm, I put on M83’s sophomore record, [_Dead Cities, Red Seas & Lost Ghosts_](https://ilovem83.bandcamp.com/album/dead-cities-red-seas-lost-ghosts).
    
    Before Nicolas Fromageau left the band and Anthony Gonzalez embraced traditional pop song structures, saxophone solos, and teen angst, M83 released two albums of mostly instrumental music. The [self-titled debut album](https://ilovem83.bandcamp.com/album/m83) is kind of forgettable, but the second one finds the French duo taking inspiration from the repetitive bombast of Mogwai and Godspeed You! Black Emperor. _Dead Cities_ is a decidedly French twist on post-rock grandeur, building blankets of sound from drum machines, analog synths, and heavily compressed guitar.

    Image: Backwoodz Studioz
    
    Billy Woods has one of the highest batting averages in the game. Between his solo records like [_Hiding Places_](https://billywoods.bandcamp.com/album/hiding-places) and [_Maps_](https://billywoods.bandcamp.com/album/maps), and his collaborative albums with Elucid as [Armand Hammer](https://armandhammer.bandcamp.com/), the man has multiple stone-cold classics under his belt. And, while no one would ever claim that Woods’ albums were light-hearted fare (these are not party records), [_Golliwog_](https://billywoods.bandcamp.com/album/golliwog) represents his darkest to date.
    
    This is not your typical horrorcore record. Others, like Geto Boys, Gravediggaz, and Insane Clown Posse, reach for slasher aesthetics and shock tactics. But what Billy Woods has crafted is more A24 than Blumhouse.

    My introduction to Sudan Archives was the song “[Nont for Sale](https://sudanarchives.bandcamp.com/track/nont-for-sale)” from her first EP _Sink_ in 2018. I’ve been a diehard fan ever since. With each album, she finds new ways to sculpt the sound of her violin, contorting it in defiance of expectations.
    
    [_Athena_](https://sudanarchives.bandcamp.com/album/athena) found her in conversation with it, leaving its timbre largely recognizable and organic, veering from experimental pop to more ambient passages. [_Natural Brown Prom Queen_](https://sudanarchives.bandcamp.com/album/natural-brown-prom-queen) embraced the aesthetics of sound collage, samples, and modern R&B, blending her violin with more expressly electronic elements. [_The BPM_](https://sudanarchives.bandcamp.com/album/the-bpm) has identifiable violin passages, but it fully embraces the more technological elements of Sudan Archives’ sound.

    ## [His House is a modern horror classic about trauma, xenophobia, and guilt](https://www.theverge.com/entertainment/839753/watch-modern-horror-masterpiece-his-house)
    
    ![His House lede image](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2025/12/His-House-lede-image.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=10%2C0%2C80%2C100&w=2400)

    There’s an argument to be made that “[the real monster is trauma](https://www.theverge.com/2018/6/7/17427554/hereditary-director-ari-aster-interview-toni-collette)” has become an overused trope in [modern horror](https://www.theverge.com/2018/11/1/18050258/new-horror-film-canon-streaming-recommendations-halloween-2018). _[Hereditary](https://www.theverge.com/2018/1/23/16924782/sundance-2018-horror-hit-hereditary-review-toni-collette-ari-aster-gabriel-byrne)_, _The Babadook_, and, much less effectively, _Smile_, are just a few higher-profile examples. But, if you ask me, few films have deployed this trope quite as effectively as the 2020 film _His House_.
    
    The film follows Bol and Rial, refugees from South Sudan, played by Sope Dirisu and [Wunmi Mosaku](https://www.theverge.com/2024/9/24/24253037/ryan-coogler-sinners-trailer-release-date). Both deliver the kind of incredible performances that you rarely see in a genre film like this. It’s part of what elevates _His House_ above the standard “haunted by trauma” fare. Mosaku, in particular, shows the sort of nuance and screen presence that in a just world would guarantee superstardom.

    ## [Manoush Zomorodi’s Bored and Brilliant is a treatise on the power of spacing out](https://www.theverge.com/report/834063/treatise-on-spacing-out-bored-and-brilliant-manoush-zomorodi)
    
    ![258151_book_CVirginia](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2025/11/258151_book_CVirginia.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C0%2C100%2C100&w=2400)
    
    ![258151_book_CVirginia](https://platform.theverge.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2025/11/258151_book_CVirginia.jpg?quality=90&strip=all&crop=0%2C0%2C100%2C100&w=2400)
    
    I have a tendency to space out. A lot. Whether it’s staring out the window on Amtrak or pausing at work to fixate on a blank spot on the wall instead of my screen, I often let my mind wander. When I was younger, I would often be derisively called a daydreamer, a space cadet, or just plain distracted. Obviously, one can be _too_ absent-minded, but _Bored and Brilliant_ by Manoush Zomorodi convincingly makes the case that letting your mind wander is not only essential, but a luxury we shouldn’t take for granted in our hyperconnected age.
    
    Zomorodi is the current host of NPR’s [_TED Radio Hour_](https://www.npr.org/podcasts/510298/ted-radio-hour?utm_source=google&utm_medium=ppc&utm_campaign=22411878927&utm_content=186155777948&utm_term=ted%20radio%20hour&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=22411878927&gbraid=0AAAAAD_p7R5H-goXXctnkI-X2zJ8QXS_r&gclid=CjwKCAiA86_JBhAIEiwA4i9Jux8xHqVey1NK2OmYdGfdVDUL6iHciTCpoNAaHLdjvb--YCCxMFYitBoCXZgQAvD_BwE), but she was also the host of WNYC’s [_Note to Self_](https://www.wnycstudios.org/podcasts/notetoself) for many years. In 2015, she did a [series of episodes](https://www.wnyc.org/series/bored-and-brilliant) on _Note to Self_ focused on removing digital distractions and the benefits of boredom. Then, in 2017, it became a book. _Bored and Brilliant: How Spacing Out Can Unlock Your Most Productive and Creative Self_ expands on those episodes, bringing in new expert voices, scientific studies, and anecdotes from Zomorodi and her audience from their own digital detox efforts.

    ## [You need to watch the found footage classic, Lake Mungo](https://www.theverge.com/column/812150/watch-the-found-footage-classic-horror-mokumentary-lake-mungo)
    
    Found footage movies are tough to pull off. For every classic like _The Blair Witch Project_ or _Rec_, there are 100 movies like _Slender_ and _Megan Is Missing_ that are, at best, forgettable, and at worst, an affront to the very concept of cinema. My personal favorite, though, is _[Lake Mungo](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=s-KnomEI6NE)_, a slow-burn mockumentary with found footage elements that manages to be both creepy and emotionally wrenching. Oh, and it’s gloriously concise at under an hour and a half.
    
    _Lake Mungo_ is, first and foremost, one of the most convincingly realized mockumentaries ever made. If you told someone it was a legit documentary about a supposed haunting, there’s a chance they’d believe you. The style is spot-on, the performances pitch-perfect, and it never overreaches. Other horror films in this style eventually go too far, resorting to bombast, like the BBC’s _Ghostwatch_ (which is also excellent). But _Lake Mungo_ remains understated, even when it shows purported footage of a ghost.

    I’ve read _a lot_ of horror books over the last two years. But my absolute favorite is easily Mariana Enriquez’ [_Our Share of Night_](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/538697/our-share-of-night-by-mariana-enriquez/). The book was originally published in 2019 in Argentina, but it only got an English translation in 2023. While it doesn’t quite reach Stephen King lengths, at nearly 600 pages, I’d say it still counts as an epic.
    
    There are certainly scary and gruesome moments in the story involving ancient gods, a powerful cult, and brutal ritual violence. But as is usually the case with the best horror, the supernatural here is a stand-in for real-world terrors. In this case, Enriquez uses the occult to explore Argentina’s history of political violence, familial trauma, and the unchecked greed of the wealthy elite.
