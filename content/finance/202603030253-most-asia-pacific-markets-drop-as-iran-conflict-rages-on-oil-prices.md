Asia-Pacific markets were mostly lower on Tuesday, as the conflict in Iran continues to rage on for a fourth day.

Oil prices [extended gains](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/01/crude-oil-futures-iran.html) after Iran reportedly said it had closed the Strait of Hormuz, with U.S. crude futures up 1.4% to $72.23, while Brent was up 1.87% to trade at $79.2 per barrel as of 9.49 p.m. ET Monday.

More than 14 million barrels per day transited via the Strait on average last year, accounting for nearly a third of the world's overall seaborne crude exports, according to Kpler data.

South Korea's [Kospi](https://www.cnbc.com/quotes/.KS11/) dipped fell 3%, dragged by 5%-plus losses in Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, but [defense players saw massive gains](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/03/south-korea-defense-stocks-soar-hanwha-aerospace-iran-us-israel-war.html), with some stocks up over 20%.

Australia's [S&P/ASX 200](https://www.cnbc.com/quotes/.AXJO/) was down 1.2%, after being one of the few markets on Monday to record a marginal gain.

Japan's [Nikkei 225](https://www.cnbc.com/quotes/.N225/) extended losses from the prior session and was down 2.29%, while the Topix dipped 2.24%.

Hong Kong [Hang Seng index](https://www.cnbc.com/quotes/.HSI/) was up 0.3%, bucking the wider downtrend while mainland China's CSI 300 lost 0.5%

Overnight in the U.S., the S&P 500 inched up 0.04% after rebounding late in the session. The [Nasdaq Composite](https://www.cnbc.com/quotes/.IXIC/) was higher by 0.36%, coming back from a 1.6% loss.

The [Dow Jones Industrial Average](https://www.cnbc.com/quotes/.DJI/) fell 73.14 points, or 0.15%, settling at 48,904.78. At its lows, the Dow was down nearly 600 points.
