The [average tax refund](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/27/average-irs-tax-refund.html) is about $350 higher so far this season, compared with about the same period in 2025, according to the latest IRS filing data.

As of March 27, the [average refund amount](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/filing-season-statistics-for-week-ending-march-27-2026) for individual filers was $3,521, up from $3,170 roughly one year ago, the IRS reported on Friday.

The IRS data reflects about 88.4 million individual returns received, out of [about 164 million expected](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-opens-2026-filing-season) through the April 15 deadline.

This season, many filers are seeing [bigger tax refunds](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/20/trump-tax-refunds.html) based on changes enacted by President [Donald Trump](https://www.cnbc.com/donald-trump/)'s "[big beautiful bill](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/one-big-beautiful-bill-provisions)" in July.

Most U.S. workers are W-2 employees, which means companies pay their taxes throughout the year via [paycheck withholdings](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/02/14/withhold-taxes-paycheck.html).

Although Trump's legislation included 2025 tax cuts, the IRS [didn't update withholding tables](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-announces-no-changes-to-individual-information-returns-or-withholding-tables-for-2025-under-the-one-big-beautiful-bill-act), and many workers overpaid taxes through the rest of 2025.

Typically, you can expect a tax refund when you overpay taxes. Alternatively, you may have a balance due when you didn't pay enough.

The size of tax refunds has been a focus for Republicans as the November midterm elections approach. The GOP has pointed to bigger average refunds triggered by Trump's tax breaks as many Americans struggle with affordability.

## How Trump's legislation is affecting returns

Nearly 50% of the tax returns filed in 2026 have included one of Trump's "signature campaign policies" — the deductions for [tip income](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/23/no-tax-on-tips-who-benefits.html), [overtime earnings](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/06/trump-no-tax-on-overtime.html), [seniors](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/23/no-tax-on-tips-who-benefits.html) and [auto loan interest](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/02/13/interest-on-new-car-loans-tax-deductible.html) — Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said in late March during a ["Fox & Friends" interview](https://www.foxnews.com/video/6392148735112).

Bessent said the [overtime deduction has been a "home run,"](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/02/trump-no-tax-on-overtime.html) claimed on 25% of returns received by the IRS, which amounted to almost 20 million filings, as of March 20.

In a Jan. 26 [release](https://www.whitehouse.gov/articles/2026/01/president-trump-delivers-largest-tax-refund-season-in-u-s-history/), the White House said the average taxpayer could [receive an extra $1,000](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/30/tax-refunds.html) or more, citing early October data from investment bank Piper Sandler. But so far, average [tax refunds have been smaller](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/20/smaller-tax-refunds.html), according to IRS data.

"We are seeing an uptick in refunds," Tom O'Saben, director of tax content and government relations at the National Association of Tax Professionals, said during a press call last week.

"I don't know that it's substantial, but one person's substantial is another person's pittance," he said.
